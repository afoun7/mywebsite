ABOUT ME

Hi, I'm Amanda.

I am currently a junior at Wellesley College majoring in computer science and minoring in mathematics. This past summer, I was a researcher at Wellesley College's Data Analytics Visualization Lab, and worked on analyzing datasets from edX courses using Python. As an aspiring software engineer, I am always eager to learn more, but I am most intrigued by the intersection of education and technology and the potential of the Internet of Things. It's important to me that more women enter the field of technology, which is why I am a member of Robogals, an organization that hosts workshops to teach young girls about computer science and programming.

When I'm not coding and designing new projects, or eating chocolate-infused treats, you're likely to find me playing my ukulele, putting in miles for races or reading Jodi Picoult books.

MY PROJECTS

Solitaire (CS230: Data Structures, final project, May 2015)

This version of Solitaire was inspired by and is very similar to the original popular card game. Some variations had to be made in order to deal with the constraints of Java's graphical user interface. For example, users must click on cards to move them instead of dragging them.

View on GitHub


StudySpot (CS220: Human Computer Interaction, final project, May 2015)

StudySpot is a semi-functional prototype, with the front-end fully implemented, of an iPhone app that helps students at Wellesley College find the perfect study space based on their work preferences. In the process of creating this project, I became familiar with Google Maps API, paper prototypes, and user testing and design.

View on GitHub


ThoughtCloud (1st Place Brandeis University Codestellation Hackathon, March 2015)

ThoughtCloud is an interface that allows users to share how they're feeling using hashtags. After users submit their thoughts, the gradient background changes colors based on their input.

View on GitHub


Jambalaya Text (CS111: Computer Programming, final project, May 2015)

Jamabalaya Text is a word game, written in Python, based on the computer game, TextTwist. Users are given a set of letters and they must try to rearrange all the letters to make as many words as they can. This is a timed (and fun!) game.
